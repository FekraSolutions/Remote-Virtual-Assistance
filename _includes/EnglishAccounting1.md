## Financial Accounting for Beginners: Definition, Stakeholders, and Core Branches

**Definition of Financial Accounting:** It is the process of recording and reporting financial transactions in companies by following accounting standards (GAAP - IFRS).

Welcome! In this short lesson, we discuss the definition of financial accounting. What is financial accounting? What are financial statements and who benefits from them? What are the main branches of accounting? We often hear terms like financial accountant, cost accountant, certified public accountant, etc. This lesson is very easy for beginners in the field of financial accounting, as well as for non-accountants interested in learning about financial accounting and its branches. The definitions we discuss today are also very useful for all accounting students in schools and universities across all curricula. ## First: What is Financial Accounting?

Financial accounting is the globally agreed-upon method for recording financial transactions in companies. It is that simple! Just as the whole world agreed that a traffic light has three colors and red means stop, the world also agreed to record all financial transactions in companies using the same method (Accounting Standards). If we think about this for a moment, it is very logical; it helps everyone in the field—managers and business owners—read and comprehend financial data, as long as it is calculated and presented the same way worldwide, right? This method is Financial Accounting.

### Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP)
Some countries establish their own specific accounting standards that apply to companies within their borders.

### International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS)
International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS) are used in many countries, especially for companies operating in more than one country, with the aim of unifying reporting on an international level. Some countries adopt IFRS entirely, while others apply local standards that are partially compatible with them.

## Second: Beneficiaries of Accounting Sciences

Why do we study financial accounting? Who needs financial reports/financial statements? Why all this effort?! A valid question, and the answer is very simple and logical. There are two types of beneficiaries of these sciences, classified as follows:

### Internal Beneficiaries
These are the internal parties within companies, specifically Management and Owners. Management benefits from accounting sciences to analyze and monitor operational performance and to make sound decisions based on accurate scientific information. Additionally, the owners (investors) who benefit from profits are stakeholders because accounting helps them make decisions regarding buying or selling companies, monitoring dividend distributions, and so on.

### External Beneficiaries
These are external parties that utilize a company's financial accounting for evaluation and monitoring purposes. Examples include tax authorities that examine company statements to determine tax liability; lenders who check statements to evaluate performance and financial ability to repay debts; and potential investors who evaluate companies to see if they are worth investing their money in.

## Third: What are the Branches of Accounting Sciences?

Certainly, accounting standards are uniform within a region or country (GAAP) as previously mentioned, and internationally (IFRS). Nevertheless, almost every company uses these standards in a way that suits its business nature and accounting goals. Does the company want financial monitoring? Cost control? Simply calculating and distributing profit? Every company has a unique operational nature, and each uses accounting differently. Furthermore, accounting specializations have branched out over time like all other sciences. There are now financial accountants, cost accountants, bank accountants, tax accountants, auditors, and many others. However, all specializations can be classified into three main branches:

### 1. Financial Accounting
A financial accountant participates in preparing a company's financial statements. This is a short sentence, but it contains many specializations. For example, the Income Statement is one of five primary financial statements we discuss in another lesson. In many companies, you see an entire accounting department specialized only in recording "Revenue," which is just half of the income statement (which matches revenues against expenses). Another example is the bank accountant, who specializes in reconciling financial transactions related to banks and matching actual bank balances with book balances. The bank account is just one of many accounts on the Balance Sheet. Thus, the financial accountant specializes in the financial reporting of events within companies.

### 2. Management Accounting
Unlike the financial accountant who follows clear and strict accounting laws and standards to report company operations legally, the management accountant has more flexibility in their work and the reports they provide. Their primary specialty (as the name suggests) is helping management make sound decisions based on actual data and statistics. A management accountant, for instance, compares the budget to actual spending, analyzes variances, their sizes, causes, results, etc. Management accounting reports are not mandatory and do not follow fixed, unified standards, but they use the most modern and accurate analytical tools possible to help the company earn more.

### 3. Accounting for Non-Profit Organizations
Some entities do not aim for profit, such as government sectors, charitable organizations, and so on. There are specific standards for these entities, as well as specialized accounting branches to report on them, given their different operational nature and their connection to specific local and international laws based on their type.
