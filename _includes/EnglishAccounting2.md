## First: The Accounting Principle (Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity)

Will we learn this equation today? No. During the course? No. During this year? Also no! If you intend to specialize in accounting, you will spend your entire career learning what this equation is and how to apply and align it in all your work! The equation is very deep and is applied in every accounting task without exception. This is not an exaggeration! We actually apply this equation in all our work. That is why it is called the Accounting Principle.

Let's start by understanding the terms. You will see, dear reader, that you have already begun to understand a lot about accounting just by understanding the terms of this equation. What are assets? What are liabilities? What is equity? And why must Assets always equal Liabilities + Equity?

### Definition of Assets in Accounting

Assets are everything the company **owns** and **uses to generate revenue on a continuous basis**. For example, the building where the work is done. If it is owned by the company and not leased, it is an asset of the company used to generate revenue. Another example is the vehicles used to deliver goods and transport employees; as long as they are owned by the company and used to generate revenue continuously, they are company assets. Another example is inventory. The company sells the inventory it owns to generate revenue; therefore, it is an asset. Cash! Yes, the company uses cash to generate revenue! Think about it... the company uses cash to buy inventory, pay salaries and rent—and why all that? To generate revenue. Therefore, cash is an asset.

* **Examples of Assets: Land - Building - Vehicle - Inventory - Cash - Patent**

### Definition of Liabilities in Accounting

Really, dear reader!? Do you want a definition for liabilities? Well... liabilities are obligations! They are the rights of others within the company. Examples include lenders, suppliers who provided us with goods but haven't been paid yet, overdue salaries, and accrued expenses. All of these are **obligations on the company that must be paid to external parties.** Right?

* **Examples of Liabilities: Suppliers - Loans - Accrued Salaries - Accrued Expenses - Miscellaneous Creditors**

### Definition of Owner's Equity in Accounting

This represents the ownership rights in the company—the rights of the owners or investors. For example, if the company owner deposited one million dollars to open the company, buy an administrative headquarters, transport vehicles, goods, and so on. What are the ownership rights of this owner? The million dollars, right? This is equity. **It is the rights of the owners (or shareholders) in the company.**

* **Examples of Equity: Capital - Par value of issued shares - Drawings - Retained Earnings**

Here, dear reader, comes the secret and the first key to understanding the Accounting Principle (**Assets = Liabilities + Equity**). Let’s think together. We agreed that assets are everything the company owns and uses to generate revenue, right? Here is the catch: quite simply and clearly, can these assets be financed by any source other than equity or liabilities? Impossible! Therefore, **Assets = Liabilities + Equity**. Because every asset in the company was either purchased and paid for (Equity) or purchased but not yet fully paid for (Liabilities). Is there a third possibility? Impossible.

In all honesty, the previous paragraph is the heart of all accounting sciences. It is the most important paragraph in the entire training course. Please read it carefully and try to absorb it as much as possible.

## Second: Explanation of the Accounting Cycle + Illustrative Example

**Definition of the Accounting Cycle:** It is the journey of recording, posting, and reporting financial events in companies following applicable accounting standards. From the initial recording in journal entries to the presentation in financial statements and the final closing entries.

As long as there is a company and business, there are financial and non-financial events occurring. As defined above, the accounting cycle is the process of recording only financial events to report them according to applicable accounting standards.

### 1- Definition of Financial Events

* Depositing capital into the bank - **Financial event, recorded**
* Hiring a CEO - Non-financial event, not recorded
* Buying a car (50% cash, 50% on credit) - **Financial event, recorded**
* Holding a board of directors meeting - Non-financial event, not recorded
* Decision to increase the CFO's salary - Non-financial event, not recorded
* Paying employee salaries for 01/2026 - **Financial event, recorded**

Is there a rule to distinguish between them? Yes: **A financial event is the transfer of financial value from one party to another.** Do not link it only to cash. For example, we can buy a patent on credit. No cash or physical assets moved, but it is certainly a financial event because we acquired an asset (the patent) and committed to paying the value later (a liability).

This is what a real entry looks like. It must contain these five columns:
1.  **Entry Number (#)**
2.  **Date:** The date of the event.
3.  **Account:** The accounts affected.
4.  **Debit / 5. Credit:** Every entry must balance (Debit = Credit).

**Understanding Debit and Credit:** The key is understanding the nature of accounts:
* **Asset Accounts** = Naturally **Debit** (e.g., Buildings, Vehicles, Inventory)
* **Liability Accounts** = Naturally **Credit** (e.g., Suppliers, Loans)
* **Equity Accounts** = Naturally **Credit** (e.g., Capital, Retained Earnings)

To increase a Debit account, you debit it. To decrease it, you credit it. The opposite is true for Credit accounts.

### 4- Preparing the Unadjusted Trial Balance

The Trial Balance is simply a **presentation of the balances of all financial accounts**:

| Account | Debit | Credit |
| :---: | :---: | :---: |
| Cash | 973,000 | |
| Inventory | 10,000 | |
| Vehicles | 50,000 | |
| Salary Expense | 2,000 | |
| Suppliers | | 25,000 |
| Equity | | 1,000,000 |
| Sales | | 10,000 |
| **Total** | **1,035,000** | **1,035,000** |

**The Golden Rules for Direction:**
1.  All Assets are Debit.
2.  All Liabilities are Credit.
3.  All Equity is Credit.
4.  All Expenses are Debit.
5.  All Revenues are Credit.

### 5- Adjusting Entries in Financial Accounting

**Definition of Adjusting Entries:** These are entries made at the end of the financial period to align financial balances with actual physical balances (e.g., inventory counts, accrued expenses).

Example: We have 10,000 in inventory in the books, but we sold some and forgot to record the cost. If we sold 5,000 worth of goods, we make an adjusting entry:

We are almost done!
1.  **Income Statement:** Revenues vs. Expenses to calculate Net Profit.

## Third: Definition of Accounting Assumptions, Principles, and Standards

Accounting is the language of business. To make it reliable, a set of rules organizes the work.

### 1. Accounting Assumptions
* **Economic Entity:** The company is separate from its owners.
* **Going Concern:** The company will continue to operate indefinitely.
* **Time Period:** The life of the company is divided into defined periods (e.g., a year).
* **Monetary Unit:** All events are measured in a single currency.

### 2. Accounting Principles
* **Historical Cost:** Assets are recorded at their purchase price, not market value.
* **Matching Principle:** Expenses must be recorded in the same period as the revenue they helped generate.
* **Full Disclosure:** All important information must be reported.
* **Consistency:** Use the same accounting methods period after period.
* **Materiality:** Focus on significant information that affects decisions.

### 3. Accounting Standards
These are detailed rules issued by bodies (like IFRS) to unify practices worldwide, ensuring transparency and comparability.

## Fourth: Introduction to Accounting Software for Beginners

With digital transformation, accounting software has replaced manual ledgers.

**What is Accounting Software?**
Computer systems designed to record, organize, and analyze financial transactions and automatically generate reports.

**Types of Modern Software:**
1.  **Simple Software:** For small projects or individuals; easy to use.
2.  **Integrated Systems (ERP):** For medium/large companies; connects all departments (Accounting, Inventory, HR, Sales).
3.  **Cloud-Based Software:** Runs online; accessible from anywhere with automatic updates.
4.  **Specialized Software:** Designed for specific industries like construction or medical clinics.
